Abstract

This study aimed (1) to explore the spatio-temporal phases of the execution of the bench press (BP) exercise based on barbell acceleration and power; (2) to describe barbell velocity, acceleration, mechanical power, and mechanical work at different load intensities; and (3) to analyse differences in kinematic and mechanical parameters. Twenty-one men (21.4 ± 1.5 years; 175.1 ± 6.7 cm; 75.8 ± 7.7 kg; 1RM: 91.7 ± 13.7 kg) and nine women (21.7 ± 2.3 years; 163.3 ± 10.8 cm; 57.2 ± 6.8 kg; 1RM: 38.9 ± 10.5 kg) were evaluated during the eccentric and concentric phases of the BP at different load intervals: interval 1 (55 to 75% 1RM), interval 2 (>75 to 85% 1RM) and interval 3 (>85 to 100% 1RM). Both temporal (duration) and mechanical variables (velocity, acceleration, mechanical power and mechanical work of the barbell) were determined using the Xsens MVN Link System. Mechanical variables were compared among the three different intervals. Interval 3 displayed greater duration compared to intervals 1 and 2. Barbell acceleration and power showed four different phases of BP movement, corresponding to the second and third phases of the exercise, bar braking (eccentric) and bar acceleration (concentric), respectively; the first and fourth phases are mainly determined by gravity instead of muscle intervention. Velocity and acceleration were different among the three different intervals during both the eccentric and concentric phases (p < 0.05). No differences were found between intervals 2 and 3 in mechanical power or mechanical work during the eccentric phase. In conclusion, the BP exercise has four phases considering barbell acceleration and power. The maximum and mean velocity and acceleration during BP performance decrease as load intensity increases. Maximum and mean mechanical power, and mechanical work, decrease progressively in the second and third intervals for both the eccentric and concentric phases. Thus, kinematics and mechanical parameters vary depending on load intensities.
Keywords:
biomechanics; concentric; eccentric; fitness; power training

1. Introduction

Weightlifting is an effective method for improving physical fitness and performance [1]. It is commonly used by athletes to enhance maximum strength, muscle power [2], hypertrophy [3] and fatigue tolerance [4,5,6]. Specifically, the bench press (BP) is one of the most widely used exercises in weightlifting by both recreational and professional athletes. Moreover, BP performance plays a key role in success in certain sports modalities, such as powerlifting, alongside squat and deadlift performance [7].
The BP is a typical training exercise used to develop muscular strength and power [8,9], which integrates a stretch-shortening cycle consisting of an immediate transition between the eccentric and concentric phases [10] during the lowering and lifting tasks. During BP execution, subjects lie supine on a flat bench, with their knees bent, and are supported at five points: both feet, gluteus, upper back and head. From this position, they lift the barbell to take it off the support, hold it with both hands, bring it down to the chest and then raise it back to the initial position, always maintaining the aforementioned five points of support [11]. Thus, the BP is considered a complex exercise, as it involves several muscles and requires high neuromuscular activity [12].
In order to evaluate this exercise, different devices have been used previously to analyse BP exercise, such as linear transducers [13,14,15,16,17], push-band [17] and 3D motion analysis [18]. These instruments measure parameters such as the mean and maximum velocity, mean and maximum mechanical power, acceleration and/or the movement time during both the concentric (barbell pushing) [13,14,15,17,18] and eccentric (barbell braking) [15,16,18] phases of the movement. Most of these parameters are considered in velocity-based training research [19], which uses velocity as a reference to evaluate training executions and adaptations. Therefore, it seems that these parameters should be considered when conducting a movement pattern analysis of the BP exercise. However, VBT does not take into account the possibility of analysing more specific (sub)phases beyond just the lowering and lifting of the barbell, which are the only phases identifiable through the linear velocity of the barbell. Despite this, a countermovement exercise consists of at least four phases, considering that there will be an acceleration of the barbell and a corresponding deceleration in each direction of the movement.
As an alternative to the systems that have been used so far, the Xsens MVN system has emerged as an inertial measurement system for analysing different movement patterns [20,21,22]. The application of the Xsens MVN system, combined with the use of structural-systemic analysis to study the locomotor apparatus as a dynamic system and the motor processes as structures [23], allows for an in-depth study of the most representative variables of muscular performance, in this case, specifically the kinematics of the barbell. The main advantage of this system is that it evaluates the linear and angular kinematics of the entire body without the limitations of optical tracking, such as marker occlusion or the restricted calibrated active volume. Regarding the analysis of the movement pattern in this training exercise, the first study on BP kinematics [24] revealed that the barbell movement followed a very similar pattern across subjects. Subsequently, Elliott et al. (1989) studied the concentric phase where the force decreases and then increases again, a phenomenon known as the “sticking region”. This phase is partly limited by the poor mechanical efficiency of the position in which the forces occur, along with the gradual loss of tension by the elastic component [25].
However, the BP exercise phases, including those where forces decrease and increase, require an in-depth study of the mechanics of movement. As an initial approximation, this study focuses on the velocity and acceleration of the barbell for a better understanding of the behaviour of the barbell prior to the whole dynamic system analysis (subject + barbell) and segments/joints kinematics and dynamics. Additionally, to delimit the approach, this study will not consider the sticking region as a specific subphase.
Given that velocity and acceleration can be affected by the intensity [26,27], it is essential to conduct a comparison and analysis across different load intensities during the BP exercise. Considering the intensity of the exercise (carried load) as an indicator of performance, it is important to distinguish between three qualities of muscular force [28]: strength, power [29] and fatigability [5], as well as between mechanical work (W) and mechanical power (P) [27,30].
Based on the above, it could be interesting to examine the BP exercise in its different phases and explore how varying load intensities affect the key parameters that influence performance during this exercise. Therefore, the aim of this study was (1) to explore the main phases of the BP exercise considering barbell acceleration and power; (2) to describe the maximum and mean velocity and acceleration in principal phases and the maximum and mean mechanical power and mechanical work of the barbell at different load intensities; and (3) to analyse whether there are differences in kinematics and mechanical parameters depending on load intensities. Thus, we hypothesised that (1) the BP exercise would consist of four different phases considering barbell acceleration and power; (2) the mean/maximum velocity/acceleration/power would decrease as the load increases, while mechanical work would increase; (3) and there would be significant differences in kinematics and mechanical parameters depending on load intensities.
The results of this study aim to complement the current methodology for evaluating the BP exercise with a valid protocol to analyse the behaviour of the barbell during the different phases of the movement. Additionally, it is expected to contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of the magnitude of measurable mechanical variables during the BP exercise, in each of its phases, under different load (intensity) conditions and the type of effort performed in each phase (concentric/eccentric). Finally, the goal is to complement VBT with other variables such as acceleration and mechanical power, so that these can be considered in the planning and monitoring of muscular training.

2. Materials and Methods

2.1. Design

A cross-sectional study was conducted to characterise the motor pattern of BP exercise based on barbell kinematic parameters and to analyse the differences in these parameters and other mechanical variables among different intensity intervals. The data were recorded through a direct-computation 1RM (One-Repetition Maximum) protocol. The One-Repetition Maximum (1RM) represents the maximum load an individual can lift in a single repetition with proper technique [31]. After determining the 1RM load, all sets’ loads were normalised (%RM) based on that 1RM. BP sets were grouped into intervals of intensity (load): interval 1 (55 to 75% 1RM), interval 2 (>75 to 85% 1RM) and interval 3 (> 85 to 100% 1RM). These intervals were selected considering the following: (1) Zatsiorsky and Kraemer [31] (p. 127) indicate that, in the Box Power Cleans exercise, execution velocities are statistically similar for loads <75% 1RM. To avoid the possible replication of this phenomenon, loads under 75% 1RM were classified as light loads (interval 1). This interval spans from 75% to 55% 1RM, as 55% 1RM was the lowest recorded load. (2) Zatsiorsky and Kraemer [31] (p. 276) also report that the NSCA recommends training with loads between 70% and 85% 1RM for advanced athletes, so loads above 75% and below 85% 1RM were classified as interval 2. (3) Loads greater than 85% 1RM were considered high (sub-maximal and maximal) and classified as interval 3.
The kinematics of the BP were recorded for each lift during the 1RM protocol at each applied load intensity. The following parameters were computed for the eccentric and concentric phases under different load conditions: (1) maximum velocity (VMAX); (2) mean velocity (VMEAN); (3) maximum acceleration (AMAX); (4) mean acceleration (AMEAN); (5) maximum mechanical power (PMAX); (6) mean mechanical power (PMEAN); and (7) mechanical work (W).

2.2. Subjects

A total of 30 healthy weightlifting practitioners were recruited for this study: 21 men (age: 21.4 ± 1.5 years; height: 175.1 ± 6.7 cm; body mass: 75.8 ± 7.7 kg; and BP 1RM: 91.7 ± 13.7 kg) and 9 women (age: 21.7 ± 2.3 years; height: 163.3 ± 10.8 cm; body mass: 57.2 ± 6.8 kg; BP 1RM: 38.9 ± 10.5 kg). The inclusion criteria were as follows: (1) age between 18 and 40 years old, (2) at least 1 year of experience in weightlifting, specifically BP, and being able to lift at least 0.75 times their body weight; (3) regular practice of the BP (three or more days per week); (4) no upper trunk injury in the last year; (5) no medical contraindication to perform maximal effort. The study was approved by the Institutional Bioethics and Biosafety Committee of the University of Extremadura (registration number: 126/2018) in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. All participants were informed about the research procedures and signed an informed consent form before starting the study. Although the sample size was relatively small, 30 participants were sufficient to achieve a statistical power of 88%, based on an effect size f of 0.25, an alpha level of 0.05, and a correlation of 0.6 among repeated measures.

2.3. Procedures

A standardised 1RM protocol was developed, following the guidelines previously described by Haff and Triplett [32]. Participants performed a specific warm-up consisting of a set of BPs with a light load that allowed them to easily perform 10 repetitions (approximately 50% of estimated 1RM). In the next set, 4 to 9 kg was added, and the subjects completed 3 to 5 repetitions. Then, another set was performed, where 4 to 9 kg was added again, and the subject attempted to complete 2 to 3 repetitions. In the following set, an additional 4 to 9 kg was added, and the subject tried to complete 1 repetition. If they succeeded, more load was added according to the subject’s judgement until they were unable to perform a complete repetition. The 1RM was considered the last load lifted when failure occurred [32], and all sets were normalised (%RM) based on that 1RM. Recovery times between sets were 5 min. Participants were encouraged to raise the barbell at maximum velocity in order to measure and compare the maximum power that could be produced at each load interval. A strength and conditioning coach supervised all participants during the warm-up and every set to ensure that the movement pattern was performed correctly Afterward, the sets in which participants worked within the established intensity ranges were recorded and compared.
The Xsens MVN Link system [33] and the MVN Analyse software [34] (2020 version) were used to collect data and record the kinematics of the entire dynamic system with the load [35]. These data were carefully stored for subsequent digital processing. Vertical position ( x → ) was calculated through mean of hands’ vertical position, while linear velocity ( v   → ) and linear acceleration ( a → ) were calculated through time derivation of vertical bar position. These data underwent a smoothing process using GCV quintic splines [36], enabling velocity and acceleration analysis without noise while preserving position magnitudes and curves, as shown in Figure 1.
Figure 1. From left to right: barbell position, velocity and acceleration of one selected sample. Red lines: raw data. Blue lines: smoothed data.
After data smoothing, mechanical power (P) and work (W) were calculated using the following equations:
P   = F   → ·   v   →
where v   → is barbell velocity, and F   → is the force according to Newton’s 2nd law F → = m   a → , where m is the load raised and a → is the acceleration of the barbell.
W is defined as the measurement of the energy transferred from one body to another over a specific period of time. In this case, muscle A performs work on segment B if the energy flows from the muscle to the segment. W is measured in joules (J) and is calculated as the integral of mechanical power (P) over the time interval [t1, t2] and the time it takes for this work to be performed; in this case, it is said that muscle A can perform work in segment B if the energy flows from the muscle to the segment. It is measured in joules [30].
W = ∫ t 1 t 2 P d t
The sets of repetitions were divided into cycles (each repetition) and phases by detecting local maxima and minima in the vertical position and velocity of the bar, resulting in four phases: acceleration of the downstroke, braking of the downstroke (eccentric phase), acceleration of the upstroke (concentric phase) and braking of the upstroke.
Only phases 2 (henceforth called “eccentric”) and 3 (henceforth called “concentric”) were analysed since these are the phases where the muscular action performed by the subject is the most important [37]. Phases 1 and 4 were not analysed because they are mainly determined by gravity forces and not by muscle action. Phase 1 can be compared to the negative impulse that happens during a countermovement jump that is not caused by the direct action of the subject [38,39], and Phase 4 is similar to its opposite because, if the concentric action were to continue until the end of the vertical path, the bar would not brake and would be thrown beyond arm’s length.
All computations, filtering and smoothing were performed using MATLAB software (version 9.8; MathWorks, Inc, Natick, MA, USA) through self-made codes after exporting the gross results from the XSENS system.

2.4. Statistical Analysis

Statistical procedures and computations were conducted using SPSS (version 25.0; IBM SPSS Inc, Chicago, IL. USA). Data are presented as mean and standard deviation (SD). The coefficients of variation (CV) for every phase of the BP exercise (mean phase duration) were also calculated. Normality and homogeneity of data were checked by performing the Shapiro–Wilk and Levene tests, respectively. Then, a repeated measure analysis of variance (ANOVA) was conducted to analyse between-interval differences in all dependent variables for every phase of the BP exercise. Partial eta squared (ηp2) was used as effect size for multiple comparison ANOVA analysis. After that, the Bonferroni post hoc test was applied for pairwise comparisons. Hedges’s g effect size was also calculated for pairwise comparisons. Effect size thresholds for Hedges’s g were determined as follows: ≤0.2, trivial; >0.2, small; >0.6, moderate; >1.2, large; >2.0, very large; and >4.0, nearly perfect [40]. Significant differences were considered for p ≤ 0.05.

3. Results

Figure 2 illustrates the description of the BP exercise movement and its different phases based on barbell position, velocity and acceleration. Based on power outcomes, the BP exercise movement can be divided into four different phases. During phase 1, there is negative vertical acceleration and velocity of the barbell, resulting in positive mechanical power (concentric). However, it has been observed that the movement of the barbell is due to the attraction of gravity rather than the concentric action of elbow flexors and shoulder extensors. Phase 2 is characterised by a positive acceleration combined with negative velocity, which indicates the performance of eccentric mechanical power and, subsequently, the eccentric mechanical work of the elbow extensors and shoulder flexors. During phase 3, there is a positive acceleration combined with positive velocity, signifying the performance of the elbow extensors and shoulder flexors, generating concentric muscle power. Finally, phase 4 is characterised by a negative acceleration with a positive velocity, which would involve the eccentric muscle power of the elbow flexors and shoulder extensors. However, it is observed that the movement of the barbell is mainly due to the attraction of gravity, not to the muscle action, although muscle activity acts partially in the development of the action.

4. Discussion

As the first objective, this study explored the main phases of BP considering barbell acceleration and power. This work described barbell velocity, acceleration, mechanical power and mechanical work at different load intensities and compared kinematics and mechanical parameters between load intensity intervals. The main findings show that BP requires a lowering phase and a lifting phase of the barbell, each divided into two sub-phases determined by the barbell’s acceleration, either in favour or against its velocity. These results demonstrate that phases 2 and 3 are critical, as they directly reflect muscular action during the braking and acceleration stages of the movement, with the countermovement occurring between phases 2 and 3 of the execution. This characterisation had not been previously described, as the available literature uses an insufficient division between the descending and ascending phases (eccentric and concentric) [41,42,43,44,45], thereby expanding the understanding of the exercise’s dynamics.
To the best of authors´ knowledge, no prior studies have described these four phases. But, similarly, the present findings from phases 3 and 4 align with other studies that differentiate between the propulsive phase and the braking phase [46,47]. In the current work, after characterising the technical movement, it has been demonstrated that the concentric phase does not encompass the entire lifting motion (rise of the barbell). This characterisation establishes a stronger connection between what other studies call the propulsive phase, and the phase 3 analysed in the present study.
Although phases 2 and 3 of the BP are key due to active muscular action, phases 1 and 4 also play a significant role in the exercise analysis and its practical application, as they reflect the overall dynamics of the subject–barbell system. On one hand, phase 1, with the negative acceleration of the barbell at the beginning of the descent, is influenced by gravity and provides information about postural preparation and initial stability. On the other hand, phase 4 involves the deceleration at the end of the ascent, highlighting the importance of an efficient transition between strength and muscular relaxation. Biomechanically, these phases reflect moments of lower muscular action but high inertial and gravitational influence. Therefore, both phases should be evaluated by combining instrumental techniques for kinematic, dynamic, and electromyographic analysis.
According to the second aim, it has also been observed that there is a decrease in the magnitude of the different kinematic and mechanical parameters as the load intensity increases, both during phases 2 and 3, aligning with the muscle mechanical model previously described by Hill [48]. Moreover, this behaviour supports the proposed hypotheses and aligns with previous studies that have observed an inverse relationship between load and velocity in weightlifting exercises [49,50,51]. However, the results show a unique case: Mechanical work decreases at each interval, despite the increase in load, which should increase the mechanical work performed. This phenomenon may be due to the methodology used to determine the phases or to the phases themselves being evaluated, as more mechanical work could be performed in phase 4 at higher load intervals. This singularity should be addressed in another more specific study. Another noteworthy aspect is that no significant differences were detected in the mechanical work performed during the eccentric phase between the adjacent intervals (1–2 and 2–3), which may be due to the variability that occurs during the performance of eccentric exercise, as it is generally characterised by less control over the precision of muscle contraction [52].
Finally, the third objective, which evaluated the differences in kinematic and mechanical parameters between different intensity intervals, has also been achieved. The findings show significant differences in key variables across the three load intervals, except for mechanical work in intervals 2 and 3 during the eccentric phase.
Regarding the comparisons between load intensities, in phase 2, the highest values across all variables were detected in interval 1, while interval 3 showed the lowest levels. The present findings are consistent with those of other studies, specifically regarding the variables VMEAN, VMAX, PMEAN and PMAX [53,54,55]. In contrast, the study by Perez-Castilla et al. [56] did not analyse the eccentric phase during lifts. This omission could be a limitation, as they compared two types of lifts but only included the eccentric phase in only one. As a result, muscular strength variables during the eccentric phase were not calculated. The present study fills this gap by examining the eccentric phase, thereby enabling a more comprehensive quantification of muscular strength variables. Similarly, Wilk et al. [57] examined the influence of cadence during the eccentric phase on barbell mechanical power and velocity in the bench press exercise. They concluded that a longer duration in the eccentric phase negatively impacts mechanical power and barbell velocity. However, their study did not examine the kinematics of the eccentric phase, which would be necessary to fully understand these effects. Importantly, their focus was solely on the concentric phase, neglecting the prior eccentric movement. Orange et al. [58] evaluated the reliability of the GymAware PowerTool system for measuring mechanical power and velocity. Yet, like many others, they overlooked the eccentric phase of the movement, missing the significant role it plays in muscle strength and performance.
Similarly to what occurs in phase 2, there is a decrease in both kinematic and mechanical parameters as the intensity of the load increases during phase 3. In line with current study findings, Pérez-Castilla et al. [56] determined that two different subphases can be identified within the concentric phase, referred to as the propulsive and braking phases, respectively. The propulsive phase is defined as the portion of the concentric phase in which the acceleration of the barbell exceeds the acceleration of gravity (≥−9.81 m·s−2), while the braking phase occurs when the force applied is lower than the barbell´s weight [46].
Considering that the kinematics of the barbell reflects the outcome of muscular action during the exercise, the results show that both propulsive [19] and mean velocity decrease as the load increases. These findings are supported by previous studies, which also observed a decline in velocity as the load increased [43]. These findings highlight the need to consider both mechanical and kinematic variables when designing training protocols based on movement analysis (such as velocity, acceleration or power).
Concerning phases 1 and 4 of the BP, these should be analysed using a more specific methodology, since an external analysis of the dynamics of the subject–barbell system, evaluating the behaviour of the barbell, is not specific enough to determine what proportion of the movement depends on muscle activity and how much on the action of gravity. To address this, an analysis of the internal dynamics of the system would be required, incorporating the characterisation of joint mechanics and electromyographic activity.
Nevertheless, some limitations of the present study should be acknowledged. Firstly, the sample size is relatively small. It would be valuable to conduct a similar study using the methodology outlined here with a larger sample to validate the findings. Additionally, the distribution of load intensity intervals is relatively broad. Although participants were instructed to push the barbell as fast as possible during each trial, this may have influenced the acceleration profile of the barbell, potentially deviating from the optimal acceleration curve principle [46]. Future research should consider defining the intensity intervals in advance and selecting narrower ranges of load intensity to achieve more precise results.
Regarding the methodological contribution of this study, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to use Xsens Link technology for BP analysis. The Xsens MVN Link system stands out as an advanced tool in biomechanical analysis due to its precision, versatility and ease of use, surpassing traditional optical systems and linear transducers. Unlike optical systems that require a calibrated space and are susceptible to marker occlusion, Xsens uses portable inertial sensors that function in uncontrolled environments, allowing for its application in real training conditions. Moreover, it has demonstrated high similarity in measured angles and positions of the segments with optical systems in dynamic tasks such as walking [16] (CMC > 0.96) and gymnastic activities. [59]. Therefore, it could be interesting to incorporate joint kinematics analysis of the BP using the methodology proposed in this study, as it provides a better understanding of the BP exercise.
This study introduces a novel methodology for the biomechanical analysis of the BP. Furthermore, it demonstrates that phases that depend on the subject’s muscular action (phases 2 and 3) are critical in determining how performance and adaptations in BP execution should be analysed.
The characterisation of the BP phases conducted in this study represents a new avenue for research on exercise kinematics based on acceleration and power, enabling the identification of distinct movement phases. Moreover, the BP characterisation presented here holds significant potential in the fields of fitness and sports training and could also be extended to areas such as physical preparation and Paralympic weightlifting. The findings of this study are particularly relevant to sports training, as they provide insights into the development of mechanical power and work, both fundamental strength qualities. Consequently, this research suggests a novel biomechanical approach to analysing conditioning techniques, motor control, and exercise physiology. In addition, there is an opportunity to pursue future research that incorporates joint biomechanics, inverse dynamics analysis, variability analysis based on dynamic systems theory [60], the uncontrolled manifold hypothesis, synergy analysis [61] and ergonomic assessments (usability, safety and comfort).

5. Conclusions

The aim of this study was (1) to explore the main phases of the BP exercise considering barbell acceleration and power; (2) to describe the maximum and mean velocity and acceleration in principal phases and the maximum and mean mechanical power and mechanical work of the barbell at different load intensities; and (3) to analyse whether there are differences in kinematics and mechanical parameters depending on load intensities. Thus, we hypothesised that (1) the BP exercise would consist of four different phases considering barbell acceleration and power; (2) the mean/maximum velocity/acceleration/power would decrease as the load increases, while mechanical work would increase; and (3) there would be significant differences in kinematics and mechanical parameters depending on load intensities.
The results confirm the first hypothesis, as the BP exercise was found to consist of four distinct phases defined by barbell acceleration and power. The second hypothesis was partially supported, as the mean and maximum velocity, acceleration and power decreased with increasing load, aligning with the hypothesis; however, mechanical work did not consistently increase across all intensities, particularly in the eccentric phase at intermediate and high loads. Finally, the third hypothesis was also confirmed, as significant differences were observed in kinematic and mechanical parameters across varying load intensities, except for mechanical work in specific load ranges, which showed no significant differences.
Regarding the contributions and novelty of the present study, it represents a significant advancement in the biomechanical analysis of the bench press (BP) by introducing the characterisation of movement into four well-defined phases based on barbell acceleration. This approach surpasses previous methodologies, which typically focus on analysing the concentric and eccentric phases as a whole, offering a more detailed and nuanced description of the exercise dynamics. In this sense, the study identifies an initial descending acceleration phase (phase 1), an eccentric braking phase (phase 2), a concentric acceleration phase (phase 3) and a final ascending deceleration phase (phase 4), highlighting phases 2 and 3 as the key moments where active muscle action is most relevant, making them critical for performance and training adaptation. This analysis not only provides a deeper understanding of the motor patterns involved in BP but also allows for a more precise assessment of the muscular and mechanical contributions at each stage of the lift.
From a methodological perspective, the use of the Xsens MVN Link system in this study represents a significant advancement. This system has proven to be a robust tool for the detailed analysis of movement phases, overcoming the limitations of traditional methods such as photogrammetry systems and linear transducers. By comprehensively characterising movement phases through high-resolution kinematic and mechanical parameters, this study establishes a foundation for future research, enabling greater precision in performance assessment and a deeper understanding of movement patterns. This approach facilitates detailed analyses of body segment and joint dynamics, as well as muscle electrical activity and muscular synergies. Furthermore, this methodology is not limited to the bench press but can also be extended to other strength exercises, contributing to the evolution of applied biomechanics in fitness.
